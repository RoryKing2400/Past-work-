ov_star %>%
  filter(brands == 'Starbucks') %>%
  group_by(NEIGHBORHOOD_DISTRICT_NAME) %>%
  mutate(star_visits = sum(visits)) %>% 
  mutate(star_percentage= (star_visits/all_visits)*(100/1))%>%
  select( NEIGHBORHOOD_DISTRICT_NAME, all_visits, star_visits, star_percentage) %>%
  unique()%>%
  arrange(desc(star_percentage)) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x = reorder(NEIGHBORHOOD_DISTRICT_NAME, star_percentage), NEIGHBORHOOD_DISTRICT_NAME, y = star_percentage)) + geom_lollipop(point.colour = 'red', point.size = 3) + coord_flip() + labs(x = 'Neighborhood', y = 'Percent of Snack or Beverage Trips to Starbucks', title = 'Breakdown by Neighborhood for Beverage or Snack Visits to Starbucks') + gghighlight(max(star_percentage)>38) + theme_minimal()

As you can see here the two lollipop graphs are almost the complete opposite. Neighborhoods like Ballard, Northwest, and Southwest that had the highest percentages of trips to Starbucks rather than other non-alcoholic beverage or snack stores, are now at the bottom of this list and vise versa. This shows that the immense popularity has driven down local/small brand coffee shops and other non-alcoholic beverage or snack stores. Where we see a lot of Starbucks’s we do not see as many smaller local stores.
